## Teardown 1: Apple (AAPL)

- **slug:** aapl-deep-dive
- **symbol:** AAPL
- **title:** Apple: The World's Most Beloved Moat
- **difficulty:** beginner
- **linked_playbooks:** [moat, margin-of-safety]
- **author:** RSJ
- **status:** draft
- **estimated_read_minutes:** 10

### The Business Model

Apple makes money three ways. First, it sells premium consumer hardware — iPhone, Mac, iPad, AirPods, Apple Watch. iPhone alone is about half the company's revenue. Second, it sells services that ride on top of that hardware — App Store fees, iCloud storage, Apple Music, Apple TV+, Apple Pay. Services are roughly a quarter of revenue, growing faster than hardware, with much higher margins. Third, a growing wearables and accessories segment.

The cycle that makes Apple special: you buy an iPhone, you get locked into the ecosystem, Apple earns from you for the next decade through services and your next iPhone. Every installed device is an annuity.

### The Moat

Apple's moat is ecosystem lock-in plus brand. Switching from iPhone to Android means losing your iMessage blue bubbles, your AirPods pairing, your Apple Watch, your App Store purchases, your iCloud photos, your family sharing. The cost of switching is emotional and practical. Combined with a brand that commands a 40% premium over comparable hardware, this moat has held for 15+ years.

Buffett bought about $150B worth of Apple because he concluded this is one of the most durable consumer businesses ever built. That's the highest compliment the most famous moat investor in history can give a company.

### Recent Earnings Snapshot

Most recent quarter: revenue around $95B, earnings per share around $1.50, services growing ~12% year over year, iPhone growing ~3%. Gross margin sits near 46% — royalty-level profitability. Apple returned roughly $29B to shareholders last quarter via buybacks and dividends. No meaningful debt problem. This is a cash-printing machine with an operator CEO.

### Three Bull Points

1. **Services flywheel.** More than 2 billion active Apple devices worldwide, each generating high-margin service revenue forever. Services revenue per device keeps climbing. This is the business Buffett cares about most.
2. **AI catalyst.** Apple Intelligence is shipping across the iPhone lineup. If it convinces the installed base to upgrade at scale, this could be the biggest upgrade cycle since iPhone 6.
3. **Capital returns.** Apple has bought back roughly 40% of its shares since 2013. Even if revenue flatlined, earnings per share would keep climbing because the denominator keeps shrinking.

### Three Bear Points

1. **China exposure.** About 17% of revenue comes from China and geopolitical friction is rising. Huawei's domestic resurgence is real. A slow-motion market-share loss in China could shave a few percent off growth every year.
2. **Innovation lull.** Vision Pro underwhelmed. The Apple Car was cancelled after 10 years and billions invested. Where does the next $100B product line come from?
3. **Valuation.** Apple trades at roughly 30x forward earnings. Historically, Apple traded at 12-18x. A multiple compression alone could mean 20-30% downside, even if fundamentals hold.

### Management Quality

Tim Cook is an operator, not a visionary — and that's exactly what a moat business needs. Since taking over in 2011 he has tripled revenue, quadrupled earnings per share, and returned more than $800B to shareholders. Criticisms about the innovation bench are legitimate, but execution and capital allocation have been textbook. Watch the CFO transition: Luca Maestri stepped down in 2025, and how the new CFO handles capital returns and China commentary will matter.

### Three Guided Questions

1. Buffett's rule: "Only buy something that you'd be perfectly happy to hold if the market shut down for 10 years." Would you hold Apple for 10 years with no ability to sell? What would have to be true about the business for you to feel comfortable?

2. What breaks the moat? Describe one realistic scenario — not a doomsday one — where the iPhone ecosystem loses its grip on consumers.

3. If Apple dropped 30% tomorrow on no company-specific news, would you buy more, hold, or sell? Your answer reveals whether you actually believe your thesis or whether you're just along for the ride.

## Teardown 2: Nvidia (NVDA)

- **slug:** nvda-deep-dive
- **symbol:** NVDA
- **title:** Nvidia: The Pick-and-Shovel of the AI Gold Rush
- **difficulty:** intermediate
- **linked_playbooks:** [ten-bagger, trend-is-friend, moat]
- **author:** RSJ
- **status:** draft
- **estimated_read_minutes:** 12

### The Business Model

Nvidia designs graphics processing units — chips originally built for video games, now the universal hardware for AI training and inference. They don't manufacture the chips themselves; TSMC does that. Nvidia's real product is the combination of silicon + software (CUDA plus a library stack) that makes those chips faster than anyone else's for AI workloads.

Today the business is ~85% data center, ~10% gaming, and a long tail of automotive, professional visualization, and robotics. Customers are the hyperscalers — Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon — followed by every enterprise trying to deploy AI. During the California gold rush, the people who got rich weren't the prospectors; they were the ones selling picks and shovels. Nvidia is that.

### The Moat

CUDA. Nvidia spent 17 years building the software layer that every AI researcher and engineer has been trained on. Switching to AMD, Google TPU, or a custom chip means rewriting your AI stack, retraining your engineers, and accepting benchmarks that are often worse. The software moat is deeper than the hardware lead.

AMD has competitive silicon on paper. It doesn't matter if the tooling is worse. Plus Nvidia's systems-level approach (NVLink for chip interconnect, InfiniBand via the Mellanox acquisition, reference server designs) makes them a full-stack supplier, not just a chip vendor. A hyperscaler ordering from Nvidia gets a complete AI factory. A hyperscaler ordering from a competitor gets boxes and a homework assignment.

### Recent Earnings Snapshot

Most recent quarter: revenue around $30B, up roughly 120% year over year. Data center revenue around $26B of that total. Gross margin ~75%. Net income roughly $17B in a single quarter. These numbers are unlike anything in semiconductor history. Forward guidance assumes continued hyperscaler capex through the next several quarters.

### Three Bull Points

1. **AI is a 10-20 year build-out.** Training frontier models, then serving inference at global scale, then building sovereign AI for nation-states, then embedding AI into robots and cars. The demand curve compounds. Nvidia is the default provider across every layer.
2. **The software moat deepens over time.** Every new AI framework, every new research paper, every new LLM targets CUDA first. Each year makes switching harder, not easier.
3. **New markets are still opening.** Sovereign AI (countries building national compute). Robotics and humanoid machines. Biology and drug discovery compute. Autonomous vehicles. Each of these is a separate multi-year market that didn't exist five years ago.

### Three Bear Points

1. **Cyclical risk.** Semiconductor companies have always been cyclical. If hyperscalers decide they've bought enough compute for the next two years and pause capex in 2026, Nvidia's revenue flatlines overnight and the multiple compresses hard.
2. **Customer concentration.** Four customers make up roughly 40% of data center revenue. If Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon all slow spending at the same time — because they usually do — the stock falls first and fastest.
3. **Competition and custom silicon.** AMD's MI300 is gaining share. Google's TPU, Amazon's Trainium, and Meta's MTIA are all in-house chips. At some scale, every hyperscaler is incentivized to build their own. Nvidia's share erodes the further out you look.

### Management Quality

Jensen Huang founded Nvidia in 1993 and has run it for 31 years. He owns roughly 3% of the company. One of the best technical CEOs alive — engineers respect him because he can talk engineering at depth. His survival of multiple near-death moments (the crypto mining crash of 2018, the gaming-only era, the attempted ARM acquisition failure) is why Nvidia is what it is today. He's obsessed with being paranoid.

The real risk here is succession. There's no obvious number two, and Jensen is 61. When he eventually hands over, the stock will test how much of Nvidia's edge was the company and how much was the man.

### Three Guided Questions

1. Lynch says a "ten-bagger" is a stock that returns 10x. Nvidia has done that several times over. Is it too late, or is the AI supercycle just beginning? Pick a side and defend it with something more specific than vibes.

2. What's the first sign that the AI capex cycle is peaking? Name three leading indicators you'd watch — things that would change before earnings actually miss.

3. If Microsoft announced tomorrow that they had a custom chip matching H100 performance, what happens to NVDA stock? Bigger question behind that: how likely do you think that scenario actually is, and how would you hedge if you wanted to stay long?

## Teardown 3: Bitcoin (BTC)

- **slug:** btc-deep-dive
- **symbol:** BTC
- **title:** Bitcoin: Digital Scarcity as an Asset Class
- **difficulty:** advanced
- **linked_playbooks:** [asymmetric-bet, trend-is-friend, ride-winners]
- **author:** RSJ
- **status:** draft
- **estimated_read_minutes:** 12

### The "Business Model" (There Isn't One)

Bitcoin doesn't have a business model. No cash flows. No earnings. No management team. No product pipeline. This is the first thing that makes it hard to analyze with the tools you use for stocks.

What Bitcoin has instead is a fixed supply — 21 million coins, ever, enforced by cryptographic rules no one can change — and a distributed global network of computers verifying transactions without any central issuer. You own Bitcoin the way you own gold: its value is whatever other people agree it's worth, backed by scarcity and adoption. The thesis is that in a world where every traditional currency can be printed at will, a provably finite digital asset plays a monetary role.

If you find that thesis silly, that's fine. Many serious people do. The point of studying Bitcoin is to understand how an asset class you may not own works — because a meaningful chunk of global wealth now flows through it.

### The "Moat"

Bitcoin's moat is network effects plus the Lindy effect. It has survived 16 years of attacks, forks, government bans, exchange collapses, crashes, and ridicule. Every year it doesn't die adds to the probability it never dies. The 21M cap cannot be changed without breaking the chain — which is the chain's whole value proposition.

No other cryptocurrency has Bitcoin's security budget (the total energy securing the network), its regulatory clarity (the US SEC approved spot ETFs in January 2024), or its brand. Ethereum is a competitor for programmable uses. Bitcoin's niche is "digital gold" — the store of value, not the compute platform.

### Recent Snapshot

Spot ETFs are live in the US and have accumulated tens of billions since approval. Institutional buyers — BlackRock, Fidelity, sovereign wealth funds — now hold Bitcoin through regulated vehicles. The April 2024 halving cut new supply issuance in half, per the protocol's built-in schedule. Historically, Bitcoin rallies 12-18 months after each halving as the supply shock plays out. This cycle either rhymes with history or breaks the pattern.

### Three Bull Points

1. **Fixed supply versus infinite fiat.** As long as central banks expand money supply, there is a case for owning a provably scarce asset. Bitcoin's supply schedule cannot be changed by any government, committee, or election.
2. **Institutional adoption is a structural shift.** BlackRock's spot ETF is one of the fastest-growing ETFs in history. Sovereign wealth funds and corporate treasuries are now allocating. This is not the retail fad of 2017.
3. **Asymmetric upside.** If Bitcoin becomes even 1% of global wealth storage, it's multiples higher than today. If it goes to zero, you lose 1x your position. For a small sizing, the payoff shape is very attractive — which is exactly what Druckenmiller's framework is built for.

### Three Bear Points

1. **Volatility is brutal.** 50-70% drawdowns have happened repeatedly in Bitcoin's history and will happen again. Most people sell at the bottom because holding through pain is much harder than they expect. The asset is only good for you if you can actually hold it.
2. **Regulatory risk is real, just not imminent.** A hostile administration could restrict self-custody, tax harshly, or constrain mining. Unlikely but not zero. Regulatory risk is highest when a crisis happens inside crypto that spills into the mainstream.
3. **The "digital gold" thesis might be wrong.** Maybe Bitcoin is just a speculative asset that fades over 20 years as the novelty wears off. The thesis depends on continued collective belief, which is not a traditional moat. Remember: assets can go to zero.

### Thesis Instead of Management

Bitcoin has no CEO. That's not a bug; it's the entire point. Satoshi Nakamoto disappeared in 2010. Core developers maintain the open-source code but cannot change fundamental rules without network consensus. The absence of a central controller is what makes Bitcoin censorship-resistant and supply-fixed.

You are not betting on a team. You are betting on an idea and its math. If you believe in that idea and can tolerate the volatility, sizing is your real decision. If you don't believe in the idea, don't own it — no amount of price action will change the underlying thesis.

### Three Guided Questions

1. Druckenmiller's asymmetric-bet rule: "If you're right you make many multiples. If you're wrong you lose 1x." Does Bitcoin fit that shape today, at current price levels? And specifically — what position size lets you survive being wrong without it hurting your life?

2. Bitcoin has crashed more than 50% multiple times. Write your rules now, before you're in a drawdown: at what percentage loss do you sell? If your answer is "I wouldn't sell," are you sure? What does your past behavior with losing positions suggest?

3. Critics say Bitcoin is a bubble. Supporters say we're early. Both can't be right forever. What specific observable data point — not a feeling — would change your view in the next 12 months?
